# SteadyMind Pilot Plan

This document defines who the first pilot users should be and how feedback should be collected.

The goal is not to run a broad beta.
The goal is to learn from a very small number of thoughtful users who fit the product well enough to produce useful signal.

## Pilot Size

Recommended first pilot:

- 2-3 users

Do not invite more than this in the first round.
At this stage, more users would create more noise than learning.

## Ideal Pilot Profile

The first pilot users should ideally be:

- comfortable reflecting on their inner life
- somewhat motivated by self-development already
- likely to use the product on phone
- able to describe what feels useful or unclear
- willing to use it for several days in a row, not just try it once

## Good Pilot Candidates

People who are a good fit for the first pilot:

- a thoughtful friend or colleague who already journals sometimes
- someone under normal mental load who wants more clarity or steadiness
- someone who is open to structured self-development without wanting therapy
- someone who can give calm, specific feedback instead of only broad opinions

## People To Avoid In The First Pilot

Avoid these profiles in the first round:

- people who are unlikely to use the product more than once
- people who dislike reflection-oriented products in general
- people who mainly give vague praise instead of useful feedback
- people currently in a very acute mental health situation where the product could feel too light or mismatched
- people who expect a polished consumer app rather than an early but serious product

## What We Want To Learn

From the first pilot users, we mostly want to learn:

- whether the product feels clear without explanation
- whether it fits naturally into a day on phone
- whether Daily Pulse feels worth repeating
- whether Foundation 30 feels meaningful and paced well
- whether Progress helps them notice anything useful
- whether they want to come back after the first few days

## Recommended Feedback Format

Keep the feedback process lightweight.

Recommended structure:

1. Invite the user with a short explanation of what SteadyMind is.
2. Ask them to use it for 3-5 days in ordinary life.
3. Collect feedback in one short conversation, voice note, or written response.

## Suggested Feedback Questions

Use these questions after a few days of usage:

1. What felt immediately clear?
2. What felt unclear or slightly awkward?
3. Did the product fit naturally into your day?
4. Did Daily Pulse feel useful, repetitive, or neutral?
5. Did Foundation 30 feel meaningful or too abstract?
6. Did Progress help you notice anything real?
7. What made you want to come back, or not come back?
8. If one thing should be improved before sharing it with more people, what would it be?

## How To Capture Feedback

Recommended first version:

- use one short interview or voice-note response after 3-5 days
- write the answers into a simple internal summary
- focus on patterns across users, not one-off preferences

Do not overbuild this yet.
We do not need survey tooling or analytics complexity for the first 2-3 users.

## Invite Message Draft

Suggested invite style:

> I’m testing an early version of SteadyMind, a calm daily mental fitness app for reflection, clarity, and direction.
> I’m looking for 2-3 thoughtful people who are open to using it on phone for a few days and then giving honest feedback.
> It’s not a broad beta yet. I’m mainly trying to learn what feels clear, useful, and worth returning to.

## What Success Looks Like

The pilot is successful if:

- users understand the core loop without much explanation
- at least some of them return more than once
- feedback is mostly about value, wording, pacing, and method
- feedback is not dominated by basic usability confusion

## Next Practical Step

The next real action is:

- make a shortlist of 3-5 possible people
- then choose the best 2-3 from that list
